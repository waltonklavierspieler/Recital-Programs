Notes

Brahms Op. 119

Brahms' final work for solo piano showcases his mastery of short-form composition. The deep relationship between Clara Schumann and Brahms is well documented (or, as well documented as possible: Brahms burned many of his correspondences and sketchbooks), and Brahms' Op. 118 and 119 seem written with Clara in mind. Indeed, Clara remarks in her diary that "it really is marvelous how things pour from [Brahms]; it is wonderful how he combines passion and tenderness in the smallest of spaces."

The first intermezzo (presented to Clara as a birthday present) shows Brahms at his most melancholic and pensive. The impressionistic opening immediately yields dissonant, extended harmonies that portend Debussy's music. Brahms on this piece says "it teems with discords. . . . it is exceptionally melancholy, and to say “to be played very slowly” is not sufficient. Every bar and every note must be played as if ritardando were indicated, and one wished to draw the melancholy out of each one of them, and voluptuous joy and comfort out of the discords. My God, how this description will whet your appetite!"

The schizophrenic second intermezzo opens with an agitated theme that is balanced by a languid waltz in the center of the short piece. Brahms demonstrates his mastery of motivic development: nearly every "new" idea is simply an imaginative and economical reworking of the opening melody.

The C major intermezzo is marked giocoso, meaning "playfully." A brief, soaring, and joyous diviertimento, it's easy to imagine Brahms employing Robert Schumann's trick: composing in/around the key of C major as a cipher representing Clara.

In the final piece of Op. 119 Brahms explores his formidably athletic style at the keyboard. a smorgasbord of technical devices is on display: jumping octaves, rich chords, hemiola, gymnastic arpeggiation, complex metric rhythm (note the opening melody is in FIVE, not four bars). Rhapsody unexpectedly in a thunderous E minor coda, as if the C minor material twice represented in the previous material has shaded the bucolic Eb major material. The choice of Eb minor is significant: the final movement of Op. 118 also explores Eb minor, though in a more jaundiced tone. One wonders if the distant key of Eb minor (with six flats in the key!) serves as the harmonic context for Brahms' ruminations at the end of his career.

In this set of short piano pieces, it's easy to hear dreams of unrequited love, dreams of the cerebral Modernist style to come, and dreams of the Romantic period that flowered and began to pass by the end of Brahms' life.

The Ballade as Narrative - Chopin Ballade No. 4

The ballade form goes all the way back to medieval poetry traditions, and have always been associated with narrative qualities: telling an epic or humorous story involving dramatic events with several actors, rather than exploring subjective or abstract ideas like the sensation of love, grief, etc..

Chopin's ballades, though not bearing any specific programmatic material to associate them with stories proper, have long been associated with the structure and content of folk tales. For instance, his second Ballade was reportedly inspired by a ballad poem by Polish poet Adam Mickiewicz. Chopin's fourth and final ballade stands as one of his finest and most complex works. The bell-like opening on the dominant C major functions as a narrator introducing the story, followed immediately by the day-dreaming main theme.

The ballade meanders through several ideas and tonal areas (Gb major, Db major, A major...), with several reoccurences of the first theme idea and the introductory bell-like narrator material returns mid-piece to announce the fiery end of the ballade. Each return of the material suggests a reoccuring character that is on a journey that inexorably alters its character...

Rzewski North American Ballads

Frederic Rzewski was one of the most interesting and diverse American composers of the late 20th century. Polemically dismissed as a "political" composer, Rzewski did not shy away from treating music as a social phenomenon that could not be divorced from its social context. As such, Rzewski drew on the sounds of American folk traditions and explicitly incorporated coeval musical and political themes into his music. These included protest music, anti-war music, and music on the lives of working people.

In these ballads, Rzewski displays blues, jazz, and contrapuntal compositional techniques. Simple settings of the folk tune being treated veer into kaleidescopic fragmentations of the theme in several keys and rates of speed at the same time. This polytonal contrapuntal technique is chaotic, yet the identity of the theme is traceable amidst the chaos. Rzewski's style here has been described as a metaphor for the inherently chaotic nature of social and political struggle (as in the protests against the Vietnam War and the struggles of exploited labor against poor working conditions).

Each piece is based on a familiar song: "Dreadful Memories" on the hardships of coal-mining; "Which Side Are You On?", a call for union organizing; "Down by the Riverside," a swords-to-plowshares hymn tune re-appropriated as anti-war song during the Vietnam War; and "Winnsboro Cottonmill Blues" on labor in a cottonmill.
